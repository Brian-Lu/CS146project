Glossary for all Genres

  • Acoustic:
    without inbuilt electrical equipment to amplify the sound.

  • Autoharp:
    a small harp with buttons to press for playing chords.

  • Bachata:
    a style of romantic Latin music that originated in the Dominican Republic.

  • Bluegrass:
    a style of country music based on old-time Appalachian music.

  • Commercial:
    made to be popular and make money.

  • Dubstep:
    electronic music that generally features sparse, syncopated drum and percussion patterns with bass lines that contain prominent sub bass frequencies.

  • East Coast Hip Hop:
    a regional subgenre of hip hop music that originated in the New York City area during the 1970s. East Coast Hip Hop music has prioritized complex lyrics for attentive listening rather than beats for dancing.

  • EDM:
    a broad range of percussive electronic music genres made largely for nightclubs, raves, and festivals.

  • Harpsichord:
    a keyboard instrument with horizontal strings that run perpendicular to the keyboard in a long tapering case and are plucked by points of quill, leather, or plastic operated by depressing the keys. It is used chiefly in European classical music of the 16th to 18th centuries.

  • Hillbilly:
    an impolite word meaning a poor mountain farmer in the USA.

  • Honky Tonk:
    a country music style known for its powerful, emotional songs.

  • House:
    genre of electronic music created by club DJs and music producers in Chicago in the early 1980s.

  • Merengue:
    a style of Latin music from the Dominican Republic that is fast-paced and has a complex two-step dance.

  • MIDI:
    Musical Instrument Digital Interface; a widely used standard for interconnecting electronic musical instruments and computers.

  • Monodic:
    written as one musical line.

  • Operetta:
    a short opera, usually on a light or humorous theme and typically having spoken dialogue. Notable composers of operettas include Offenbach, Johann Strauss, Lehár, and Gilbert and Sullivan.

  • Organum:
    two or three lines moving simultaneously but independently, therefore almost inadvertently representing the beginnings of harmony.

  • Salsa:
    a style of Latin music and dance that incorporates Afro-Cuban rhythms; originally from Cuba, but is now produced by many Latin American countries.

  • Samba:
    a style of Latin music and dance that is typically seen at Brazilian festivals and is associated with dancers wearing elaborate floral costumes.

  • Scratching:
    sometimes referred to as scrubbing, is a DJ and turntablist technique used to produce distinctive percussive or rhythmic sounds and sound effects by moving a vinyl record back and forth on a turntable while optionally manipulating the crossfader on a DJ mixer.

  • Synthesizer:
    a musical instrument that generates sound using oscillators, filters, sequencers, and other things of that nature; most synthesizers are controlled with three things: a keyboard, pitch bend and modulation wheels, and a complex interface of knobs and switches.

  • Trance:
    a style of uplifting and hypnotic electronic music.

  • West Coast Hip Hop:
    a hip hop music genre that encompasses any artists or music that originate in the West Coast region of the United States. The gangsta rap subgenre of West Coast Hip Hop began to dominate from a radio play and sales standpoint during the early 1990s with the birth of G-funk and the emergence of Suge Knightand Dr. Dre’s Death Row Records.
